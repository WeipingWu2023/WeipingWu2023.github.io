The PE RATIO Explained! - Price To Earnings Ratio Made Simple!

|Post Views:

Summary

Link Text

The video explains the Price to Earnings (P/E) Ratio—one of the most popular metrics used in investing—in a very simple way. Essentially, it is a quick way to see whether a company is good value for money.

Here is a super easy summary of what you need to know about the P/E ratio:

1. What is the P/E Ratio?

The P/E ratio is just Price over Profit.

  • P stands for the Share Price (how much the company is asking you to pay for one share).
  • E stands for Earnings Per Share (EPS), which is just another way of saying Profit per Share.

When you calculate it, the resulting number tells you how many years it would take to get your money back based on the company’s current profits.

In general, a lower P/E is better. For example, if a company has a P/E of 10, it means it would take 10 years of current profit levels to earn back the price of the stock, which is much better than a company with a P/E of 40.

2. How Do You Use It?

A P/E ratio by itself (like 35 for Apple) doesn’t help much. It becomes useful only when you compare it to other things:

  • Competitors: You can compare it to similar companies (e.g., comparing Apple’s P/E of 35 to Microsoft’s P/E of 34 suggests Microsoft is a slightly better value).
  • The Sector: You can compare it to the average P/E of the entire industry (or sector) the company belongs to (e.g., if the technology sector P/E is 42, then both Apple and Microsoft look like good value).
  • Historical Data: You can look at how the company’s P/E has changed over many years.

3. Different Types of P/E Ratios

You might hear three terms related to P/E:

  1. Trailing P/E: This uses the company’s actual profit data from the past 12 months. It’s good because it uses real numbers, but the drawback is that it’s old data.
  2. Forward P/E: This uses profit that the company is projected to make over the next 12 months. This might be more accurate for the future, but projections aren’t always what the company actually achieves.
  3. Schiller P/E (or Cyclically Adjusted P/E): This ratio is designed to smooth out temporary ups and downs. It calculates the P/E by taking a 10-year average of profits and adjusting for inflation. The Schiller P/E is often used to see if the entire stock market (like the S&P 500) is historically overvalued or undervalued.

4. Important Warnings and Limitations

The P/E ratio is not perfect.

  • It shouldn’t be used alone.
  • It works best for established companies.
  • It is not very useful for newer, modern tech companies that may be unprofitable for many years while they are still growing (like Tesla). If a company has little or no profit, but its share price is high, the P/E ratio can jump to an extreme number (like 800 for Tesla at one point). This happens because investors are betting that the profits will increase greatly in the future, which will quickly drop the P/E ratio.

To understand the P/E ratio, think of it like comparing different items at a supermarket: If you want to know which product offers the best value, you don’t just look at the price (Share Price); you have to look at the price relative to how much usable product you get (Profit). A lower ratio of price to profit usually means a better deal.

The P/E Ratio: What It Is and How to Use It

Hey guys, welcome back. Now if you have anything to do with investing, you probably keep hearing about the P/E ratio. It’s referenced everywhere from all the different finance websites, videos, analysts, podcasts. But you might be thinking: what exactly is a P/E ratio? What is a good P/E ratio? And how do we actually use it to make better investing decisions?

Well, the purpose of this video is to demystify and simplify the price to earnings ratio, but more importantly help make it a usable metric. Essentially, it’s a quick way to see whether a company is good value for money — but it’s not perfect, as you will soon discover.

As it is one of the most popular investing metrics used, I did want to bring it all together in a quick 10 minute video. So by the end of this video you will know what a P/E ratio is, what a trailing P/E ratio is, what a forward P/E ratio is, what the Shiller P/E ratio is, and even more importantly, how to use it to make you a better investor.

As always, if you do find anything in this video useful, be sure to drop a like — that would be much appreciated, as every like does help. Okay, sit back and relax guys, here is what you need to know about the P/E ratio.

What’s Coming Up

Okay so here is what we’ve got coming up. We’re first going to discuss the problem — so why do we even have the P/E ratio? We’ll then dig into it and find out exactly what it is. We’ll then take a look at some stock analysis of a couple of examples. And then we’ll finish with the Shiller P/E and some of the cool things we can do with that.

The Problem: Why Do We Even Have the P/E Ratio?

Well, here’s my question to you. Which company is the better investment — company A, and its share price is a thousand dollars, or company B, and the share price is a hundred dollars?

Now at first glance you might look and go, well I can get 10 times the amount of shares from company B than company A, so probably company B. However, there is one major thing missing from this piece — and arguably what is the most important thing in a business — profits. How much profit is the business making?

What Is the P/E Ratio?

So what is the P/E ratio? Well, the P stands for the share price and the E stands for earnings per share, or EPS. Now earnings is just another way of saying profit. So a simpler way to look at this is it’s price over profit — price: how much is the company asking you to pay, and the profit: how much profit is there in each individual share.

Now you have both sides of the equation. So let’s put this in a quick example. We’ll use Apple as it’s a company that everybody knows and likes. So last quarter its share price was $115, its earnings per share was $3.28, so this gave us our first P/E ratio which is 35.

So two quick things. How you get the 35 is 35 times 3.28 gives you the share price, and this is why it’s known as a multiple. And the second thing is that it’s going to take you 35 years to get your money back. So in general, a lower P/E is better.

How to Use the P/E Ratio: Making Comparisons

Okay so now we have our metric, we’ve got it — 35. But what does that mean? A P/E ratio by itself doesn’t really help you. You now have a metric that you can compare it to other things, and this is where the P/E ratio is really usable. It’s: what do you compare it to?

The most obvious thing would be its closest competitor. So let’s take a look at Microsoft. Share price was $210, its earnings per share was $6.20, so this gave a P/E of 34. Okay so now we have a comparison, and we can see all things being equal, Microsoft does look like the better investment. It’s going to take you less time to get your money back, and you can see they’re making more profit per share that they’re offering to investors.

But we can take it a step further. It’s even more useful when you can compare it to the sector itself. So these companies fit into the technology sector — so what is the P/E of all the companies added together? Well as it stands it was 42. So now this changes the context again. Now Apple and Microsoft look like good value for money in comparison to the technology sector, and the difference between the 34 and 35 is now negligible.

Trailing P/E vs. Forward P/E

Some quick terminology. So you might be hearing about the trailing P/E and also the forward P/E. It’s very simple. The trailing is basically using the data from the previous 12 months, and the forward is projecting what it’s going to do over the next 12 months.

Now there’s pros and cons to each. The trailing is really good because that is what the company actually did, but the downside is that this is in the past. With the forward, it may be more accurate because they’re projecting what they’re going to do. However, the downside is what a company says it’s going to do and what a company actually does can be very different.

Real Examples: Apple and Tesla

So let’s take a look at Apple, and I’ll bring up the P/E ratio of the last 12 years. Again, once you see over a longer time frame I think it becomes more useful. So you can see 12 years ago it was breaking that 30 mark, it was then trading in the 10 to 20 range for quite a long time, and it’s this last year where the P/E ratio has jumped up.

But remember two things make up the P/E ratio — the share price and also the profit per share. So I find it quite useful to take a look at all the different metrics. Now you can understand why the P/E ratio has jumped, because the earnings per share is pretty steady — it’s had a nice good growth rate so that looks great — but it’s the share price that has jumped in the last year, and this is why the P/E ratio has also doubled and tripled. So the P/E ratio is really good for established companies.

Okay let’s take a look at Tesla, and we’ll bring up the P/E ratio. And you can see not a lot happened for 12 years, and then all of a sudden it’s jumped up, and Tesla has a P/E ratio of around 800. So what is going on here?

Well let’s bring up the stock price and also the earnings per share. So because it’s factoring in profit, you can see on the earnings per share Tesla has been unprofitable for a lot of years, and it’s only now breaking into the profit. This is why the P/E ratio was zero, and you can see the stock price has jumped up this last year, sending the P/E ratio to 800.

So why would an investor buy this? They’re not going to wait 800 years to get their money back, right? Well basically, investors are betting that Tesla’s profits are going to be increasing considerably over the next few years, and this will drop the P/E ratio considerably.

So I have found that the P/E ratio is not so good for these modern tech companies, where companies get launched on millions and millions of investors’ money and they’re unprofitable for many, many years. The P/E ratio doesn’t really help.

The Shiller P/E

Next up, the Shiller P/E. So what is the Shiller P/E? Well this was created by Robert Shiller of Yale. It’s also known as the cyclical adjusted P/E, because what he did is he takes a 10-year average of the profits. And this means if there’s one year that spikes up or dropped down, it doesn’t mess up the whole chart, as he’s taking a bigger average. And he also adjusts it for inflation.

So let’s look at the P/E ratio by sector, and remember it’s always more useful to have a comparison. So right in the middle there is the S&P 500 — so we can see right now it’s just above 30. And now we can see there may be opportunities in the energy sector as this has a very low P/E relative to the market, and it’s saying that real estate is really high.

So this chart is the S&P 500 over a much bigger time frame — this is 120 years, and I do love a nice long term chart. So you can see here it really peaked in 1929 before the crash, also the dot-com in the year 2000. It also shows the all-time historical average is going to be around 17, and the P/E right now is around 33. So you can see historically it’s traded in this 10 to 20 mark, but more recently we’re now moving into a 20 to 30.

But this is a great way to see when the stock market as a whole is either overvalued or undervalued. Compare this to the regular P/E, and you can see that there was just a huge spike in 2008 — this was the financial crisis, and companies’ profits collapsed, which is why there was a big spike. But you can see the Shiller P/E becomes much more useful.

Bringing It All Together

So this brings it back to the first question: which is a better investment? We have company A and its share price is one thousand dollars, or company B with its share price of a hundred dollars. But this time you know that company A has a P/E of 10 and company B has a P/E of 40. And you also know the historical average being 17, and the current one is 33.

So you can now see that company B with a P/E of 40 is way above the historical average — it’s a lot higher than the market as a whole — in comparison to company A at 10, which is lower than the historical average and a lot lower than the current P/E. So even though the share price is more, company A is going to be a better investment.

Wrapping Up

So there you are guys, I hope this has helped you understand a lot more about the P/E ratio. Essentially people use it to see whether a company is good value for money, and as you saw it’s also a great way to take a look and analyze sectors and also the entire stock market as a whole. And in this case the Shiller P/E does a better job.

Just bear in mind that the P/E ratio should never be used just by itself, especially nowadays when a lot of companies are launched with millions and millions of dollars of venture capital and they can be unprofitable for years and years and years. It’s more useful for established companies.
